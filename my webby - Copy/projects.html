E-design 314 Light System

During third year, our first E-design module involved the development, testing and reporting of a multi-modal light system with analog and serial control. The project involved circuitry but focused on developing low-level software and understanding technical documentation of different parts. This project involved UART, I2C, signal processing, and many other techniques detailed in the report below.

A large challenge I faced involved understanding the limitations and low-level bugs one can face. Some of these bugs involved timing issues, issues regarding interrupts and hardware constraints. This was made especially difficult to tackle in a growing code base.

The project culminated in a multi-functional light system that was assessed through automated testing stations, written tests and technical reports. Achieving 73%, this project improved my skills in low-level programming skills, research and technical report writing, and more broadly my problem-solving and design.

E-design 344 Health Monitor

The second E-design course of third year saw us creating a predominantly analog health-monitoring system. This involved making a pedometer, weight sensor, heart rate sensor, and temperature sensor with basic elements which were processed via a microcontroller and outputted via Wifi to a web server or directly to a screen. These simple components meant we were required to read through many datasheets, complete sufficient field research, understand governing theory and equations of the components, consider project specifications, and create custom circuit designs. These circuits had to measure the sensors value and often filter and amplify the signal. Each stage involved a literature review, creating a design, simulating that design, then implementing, testing and tuning it, and finally reporting on this. When designing, we had to consider the hardware limitations and ensure that the circuit would be robust to noise. Achieving a mark of 86%, I improved my skills in analog design, sensor interfacing, research on components, and more broadly in problem-solving and design.

Hobby Electronic Projects

In leisure time I enjoy delving into electronic projects, often using microcontrollers to bring ideas to life. Projects of note include a model parking sensor using a buzzer and ultrasonic sensor, and a temperature-dependent motor controller operating an angular heater switch. I also love to learn whenever I can, learning from electricians and any engineers at work.
A major undertaking involved programming and creating hardware for individually addressable RGB LED strips. I crafted diverse patterns, including a cozy fireplace effect, on a 6-meter strip. I also developed a button-controlled ping pong game, where players timed presses to return the ball on the dynamic LED strip.
To ensure optimal current supply and brightness, I connected multiple USB ports in parallel with diodes to ensure forward flow, and ensured sufficient wire sizes. To reduce color drop, I injected power in series to different points on the strip to tackle resistance along the bus.
Next, I employed an ESP32 to make the strip Wifi controllable. I created an aesthetic local web server which can work with or without Wifi. The server allows users to select patterns, custom colors, speeds and other parameters. My largest mode is a realtime music visualizer. A python script is used on the user's PC to perform Fast Fourier transforms on the current music, break it into frequency bands, analyze the volume of each sub-band, and test for an energy spike in a specific low frequency sub-band (a beat). This is all done with band regularization. This is sent via UART to the ESP32 which visualizes the volumes and beats on the LED strip.
Further improvements could be using a sophisticated switching power supply for more consistent voltage and current and a metal rail with a filter for aesthetics and thermal regulation. Further, I could employ a high-power port system to make connections easier.

Speaker Amplifier

In our electronics module in third year, I worked on an incredibly rewarding project. Building the amplifier involved research on different filtering and high-power amplification techniques. The amplifier was specified to supply 24W of power to an 8 ohm load. The amplifier also required bass and treble control and to be fully analogue with basic circuit elements. The amplifier turned out a massive success and was able to fill the entire lab with rich and loud sound. The clarity was excellent across the entire frequency spectrum, far surpassing the quality of even my JBL Charge-4 speaker.
