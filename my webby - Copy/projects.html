E-design 314 Light System

In my third-year, first semester, E-Design module, I underwent a self-guided journey of designing an electronic device, blending disciplines such as circuit design, hardware implementation, and an expansive software component. Rigorous testing phases, assessments, and technical reports sharpened my problem-solving, design, and programming skills.

The device, controlled via UART protocol, push buttons, an analog slider, and a multi-gesture trackpad, calibrated data to configure specific outputs. Outputs included an adjustable intensity high-powered white LED with flashlight, strobe, and morse code modes, and an adjustable RGB LED.

Further information on the hardware and software schematics, attached below, is detailed in the report. The project, for which I achieved an 85% score, provided invaluable exposure to practical problem-solving, circuitry design, efficient algorithm development, and effective software debugging practices, significantly enhancing my electronics knowledge and coding skills.

Hobby Electronic Projects

Engaging in electronics projects has become my hobby, utilizing Arduino and STM32 microcontrollers. Notable projects include a model parking sensor with a buzzer and ultrasonic sensor, as well as an temperature dependent auto-switching motor controlling an angular switch heater switch.
A major undertaking involved addressable RGB LED strips. I crafted diverse patterns, including a cozy fireplace effect, on a 6-meter strip. Moreover, I developed a button-controlled ping pong game, where players timed presses to return the ball on the dynamic LED strip. Addressing limited component access, I repurposed old USB cables for power transmission at 5V, reaching for about 1/4 of the maximum sink of 21.6A. I connected the cables in parallel with diodes to prevent differential voltage reverse emf Serial power injection tackled bus resistance, upholding consistent voltage levels. With proper wire sizing and soldering, I prevented any current bottleneck. My focus centered on surpassing component limitations, optimizing brightness, and ensuring seamless operation in LED strip projects. This task was successful however in future I would like to add more current, for higher brightness, and control thermal issues with a metal rail which also allows for discrete wiring. A filter should also be placed in front for even light dispersion across the strip.

Speaker Amplifier

This task was done as part of the electronics 3rd year project. It involved learning about different filter and high-power amplification techniques. The amplifier was specified to supply 24W of power to an 8 ohm load. The amplifier also required bass and treble control and was completely analogue. The amplifier turned out a success and was able to provide extremely loud sound and excellent clarity on low and high frequencies, far surpassing the quality of my JBL speaker.

E-design 344 Health Monitor

This project was done as per requirements for our third year second semester e-design module. This module was focused on an analogue design of a health monitoring system. The system involved a pedometer, weight sensor, heart rate sensor, and temperature sensor. These components were all bare-boned analogue components and required conditioning to the signals to improve signal-to-noise ratio, improve clarity, and alter waveforms. The system was designed to be low-powered. Some software design was made to ensure readable user output. The central processor controlled output to the OLED screen and wireless communication via Wifi to a user server.
