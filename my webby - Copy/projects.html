E-design 314 Light System

In my third year's opening semester, I tackled the demanding E-Design 314 project, aiming to develop a multi-modal light system with control through analog inputs and serial communication. While the project did involve circuitry, it primarily focused on developing low-level software and understanding technical documentation. This project involved UART, I2C, analog signal processing, and many other techniques detailed in the report below.

A significant challenge was overcoming strange bugs which often related to hardware constraints, timing issues, and other factors. This was made especially difficult in a growing code base.

The project culminated in a multi-functional light system that was assessed through automated testing stations, written tests and technical reports. Achieving 85%, this project enriched my software ability, low-level programming, technical report writing, and broadly my problem-solving and design skills.

Hobby Electronic Projects

In leisure time I enjoy delving into electronic projects, employing microcontrollers to bring ideas to life. Projects of note include a model parking sensor using a buzzer and ultrasonic sensor, and a temperature-dependent motor controller operating an angular heater switch.
A major undertaking involved individually addressable RGB LED strips. I crafted diverse patterns, including a cozy fireplace effect, on a 6-meter strip. Further, I developed a button-controlled ping pong game, where players timed presses to return the ball on the dynamic LED strip.
To ensure optimal current supply and brightness, I connected multiple USB ports in parallel with diode protection whilst considering wire sizes. To reduce color drop, I injected power in series to different points on the strip to tackle resistance along the bus.
Next, I employed an ESP32 to make the strip Wifi controllable. I created an aesthetic local web server which can work with or without Wifi. The server allows users to select patterns, custom colors, speeds and other parameters. Most notably is the realtime music visualizer. A python script is used on the users PC to perform Fast Fourier transforms on the current music, break it into frequency bands, analyze the volume of each sub-band, and test for an energy spike in a specific sub-band, or a beat. This is all done relative to other samples to ensure consistency. This is sent via UART to the ESP32 which visualizes the volumes and beats on the LED strip.
Further improvements could be using a sophisticated switching power supply for more consistent voltage and current and a metal rail for aesthetics and thermal regulation. Further, I could employ a high power port system to make connections easier. A dispersion filter would further improve the aesthetics.

Speaker Amplifier

This project was done as part of my third year electronics module. It involved learning about different filtering and high-power amplification techniques. The amplifier was specified to supply 24W of power to an 8 ohm load. The amplifier also required bass and treble control and was fully analogue. The amplifier turned out a success and was able to provide extremely loud sound and excellent clarity on low and high frequencies, far surpassing the quality of even my JBL Charge-4 speaker.

E-design 344 Health Monitor

Our third year, second-semester E-design project saw us creating a predominantly analog health-monitoring system. This involved a pedometer, weight sensor, heart rate sensor, and temperature sensor which were processed via a microcontroller and outputted via Wifi to a web server or directly to a screen. These simple analog components required us to read through datasheets, understanding governing theory and equations of the devices, consider project specifications, and create fully unique circuit designs. These circuits had to logically read the sensors value and often filter and amplify the signal. Each stage involved a literature review, creating a design, simulating that design, and finally implementing and testing it. When designing we had to consider the hardware limitations and ensure that the circuit would be robust to noise. The circuit also had to be calibrated for tolerances. Overall, I learnt valuable skills and received x%.
