E-design 314 Light System

In my third year's opening semester, I tackled the demanding E-Design 314 project, aiming to develop a multi-mode light system with control through analog inputs and serial communication. While the project did involve circuitry, it primarily focused on developing software and understanding technical documentation, bridging the gap between low-level hardware manipulation and high-level software development.

Modular coding was vital for maintaining the system's flexibility, facilitating updates, and streamlining troubleshooting. Overcoming complex and peculiar bugs amid processor constraints was a significant challenge, reinforcing the value of best coding practices, the clarity that comes with a fresh mind, and the necessity of hardware consideration in programming.

The project culminated in a multi-functional light system that was assessed through automated demonstrations and evaluation of our technical reports. Achieving 85%, this project enriched my skills software and hardware while improving my problem-solving and design skills.

Hobby Electronic Projects

In leisure time I enjoy delving into electronic projects, employing Arduino and STM32 microcontrollers to bring ideas to life. Projects of note include a model parking sensor using a buzzer and ultrasonic sensor, and a temperature-dependent motor controller operating an angular heater switch.
A major undertaking involved addressable RGB LED strips. I crafted diverse patterns, including a cozy fireplace effect, on a 6-meter strip. Moreover, I developed a button-controlled ping pong game, where players timed presses to return the ball on the dynamic LED strip.
The LEDs had a large current requirement, and to save costs I connected multiple USB ports together with diode protection into sufficient wire sizing. Serial power injection tackled bus resistance, upholding consistent voltage levels. This ensured optimal brightness and prevented line color loss.
Next, I made the strip Wifi controllable. I created an aesthetic local web server on the ESP32 microcontroller, allowing for control of the LEDs through a web browser.
Further improvements could be using a sophisticated switching power supply for more consistent voltage and current and a metal rail for aesthetics and thermal regulation. A dispersion filter would further improve the aesthetics.

Speaker Amplifier

This task was done as part of the electronics 3rd year project. It involved learning about different filter and high-power amplification techniques. The amplifier was specified to supply 24W of power to an 8 ohm load. The amplifier also required bass and treble control and was completely analogue. The amplifier turned out a success and was able to provide extremely loud sound and excellent clarity on low and high frequencies, far surpassing the quality of my JBL speaker.

E-design 344 Health Monitor

Our third year, second-semester E-design project saw us creating a predimonantly analog health-monitoring system. This involved a pedometer, weight sensor, heart rate sensor, and temperature sensor and output through a microcontroller and screen. These simple analog components required us to read through datasheets, understanding governing theory of the devices, consider project specifications, and create unique circuit designs. These circuits would intepret the signals in a logical way and condition the signal-to-noise ratio. The project involved reading up on theory, creating a design, simulating that design, and finally implementing it. When designing we had to consider the hardware limitations and ensure that the circuit would be robust to noise. The circuit also had to be calibrated for tolerances. Overall, I learnt valuable skills and received x%.
